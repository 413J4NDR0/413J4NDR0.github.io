About Me

As I mentioned on my homepage, I am Alejandro Ortega, currently a senior at Duke University studying Mathematics and Computer Science. For a TLDR: I was born in México but grew up in the San Francisco Bay Area, mostly in the City of South San Francisco. If you are for some reason interested in more details, continue reading.

I was born in Irapuato, GTO, México in May 1998, and spent the first two years of my life on my paternal family's farm in Pénjamo, GTO, México. Both of my parents are from Pénamo, and the majority of my family continue to live in the city. On a mild tangent, people born in Irapuato are known as freseros because the city is famous for its strawberries, and Pénjamo is known as la cuna de la independencia since the city is the birthplace of Don Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla, who began the Mexican War of Independence against Spanish colonial rule in 1810. Unsurprisingly, I do not remember much from this time.

My father has a long history with San Francisco, and decided to permanently move us to the Bay Area in early 2001. We settled in South San Francisco, CA, known as South City to locals. Living on the border of South City and San Bruno, we were ten minutes away from both Tanforan shopping mall and YouTube's headquarters. On another tangent, during World War II, Tanforan—then a racetrack—served as a processing center for Japanese Americans of the Bay Area as a result of Executive Order 9066 that began the unconstitutional internment of American citizens on American soil. Having only ever called the Bay Area home, these Americans were forcibly relocated to internment camps and had their properties forfeited and seized; at Tanforan, they slept in horse stables, bleachers and hastily built barracks, before they were processed and sent to camps for the remainder of the war. My family would often go to Century Plaza 10 to watch movies, when fifty years earlier a blatant disrespect for human rights was occurring on the other side of the street. Of course, this is mostly a forgotten story even in my city, and certainly not acknowledged by out-of-towners chasing profits at tech companies.

I actually didn't know I lived so close to YouTube's headquarters until much later. For the most part, the only influence the tech sector had on my family was the high cost of living, which in recent years has skyrocketed to unreasonable extents. I remember my father picking up old—and free—computers from the local Goodwill that ran operating systems several years older than myself. Needless to say, I had very little exposure to technology despite growing up in the Bay Area.

My family moved to Richmond, CA in early 2005, a major city in the Bay Area that is the northern-most point of the East Bay. For people unfamiliar with the geography of the Bay Area, San Francisco is connected to Oakland via the Bay Bridge, and Richmond is some 35 minutes north of Oakland. Unfortunately, the three years we lived in Richmond coincided with a massive increase in crime that placed the city in the top five nationally for murder rate. Perhaps the only good to have come from this time was the birth of my sister, who is featured in my profile picture.

Eventually, my parents cut their losses and we moved to Hayward, CA, in 2008, a major city some 30 minutes south of Oakland in the East Bay. However, we would only spend about a year in Hayward before returning to South San Francisco in 2009, where we have been since.

Unlike the overwhelming majority of my peers at Duke University, a distinguishing feature of my upbringing was the diversity of my schools and my friends:

  • Los Cerritos Elementary in South City was about 60% Latino and 30% Asian (K-2);
  • Martin Luther King, Jr. Elementary in Richmond was about 60% Latino and 30% black (3 months of 3rd Grade);
  • St. Cornelius Catholic School in Richmond was about 90% Latino, and the remaining 10% was Asian or black (4-5);
  • Southgate Elementary in Hayward was about 45% Latino, 35% Asian and 10% black (6);
  • Martin Luther King, Jr. Middle in Hayward was about 65% Latino, 20% Asian and 10% black (3 months of 7th Grade);
  • Parkway Heights Middle in South City was about 80% Latino and 15% Asian (7-8);
  • El Camino High in South City was about 40% Latino and 50% Asian (9-12)

Also unlike my peers at Duke University, I attended seven schools before matriculation, a number that still seems astounding. Although attending a University that is about 48% white was more of a culture shock than I could have anticipated, I have enjoyed my time at Duke University immensely.
